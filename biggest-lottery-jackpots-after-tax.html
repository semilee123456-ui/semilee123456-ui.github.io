Why does the cash payout ratio jump around so much?

The lump sum vs. advertised-jackpot ratio depends mainly on prevailing interest rates at the time (it's ranged from about 44% to 62% across these five draws). It's not really about the jackpot size itself — it's a present-value calculation, and rates were simply higher or lower at each drawing.

Would a foreign winner take home more or less?

It depends. A foreign (nonresident alien) winner gets a flat 30% withheld by the US at the border regardless of bracket — which by itself is actually a lower rate than the 37% a US resident pays on a jackpot this size. But foreign winners typically still owe tax back in their home country on top of that (this calculator was originally built around exactly this question for Korean residents, who face an extra layer of home-country tax that a foreign tax credit only partly offsets). Once home-country tax is added in, a foreign winner very often ends up keeping less than a US resident would — sometimes a lot less, depending on the country. Ranked by the actual lump-sum cash payout winners chose to take, not the advertised annuity jackpot. Only amounts confirmed by official lottery statements or news reporting are used.

A foreign (nonresident alien) winner gets a flat 30% withheld by the US regardless of bracket, which is actually lower than the 37% a US resident pays on a jackpot this size. But foreign winners typically also owe tax back in their home country on top of that, only partly offset by a foreign tax credit — so once home-country tax is added in, a foreign winner very often ends up keeping less than a US resident would.

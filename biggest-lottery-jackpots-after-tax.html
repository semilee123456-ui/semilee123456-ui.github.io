Why does the cash payout ratio jump around so much?

The lump sum vs. advertised-jackpot ratio depends mainly on prevailing interest rates at the time (it's ranged from about 44% to 62% across these five draws). It's not really about the jackpot size itself — it's a present-value calculation, and rates were simply higher or lower at each drawing.

Would a foreign winner take home more or less?

It depends on the country. A foreign (nonresident alien) winner gets a flat 30% withheld by the US at the border regardless of bracket — which by itself is actually a lower rate than the 37% a US resident pays on a jackpot this size. But foreign winners typically still owe tax back in their home country on top of that, only partly offset by a foreign tax credit. A China resident ends up ahead of everyone here: China's flat 20% rate on lottery winnings is already below the 30% the US withholds, so the credit fully absorbs it and nothing more is owed. An India resident owes a bit more, since India's rate (with surcharge and cess) works out to roughly 39%, just above the US withholding. A Korea resident owes the most on top, since Korea taxes it as ordinary income at progressive rates up to 45% — this calculator was originally built around exactly that question. Frequently asked questions

How is this ranking calculated?

Ranked by the actual lump-sum cash payout winners chose to take, not the advertised annuity jackpot. Only amounts confirmed by official lottery statements or news reporting are used.

Would a foreign winner take home more or less than a US resident?

A foreign (nonresident alien) winner gets a flat 30% withheld by the US regardless of bracket, which is actually lower than the 37% a US resident pays on a jackpot this size. But foreign winners typically also owe tax back in their home country on top of that, only partly offset by a foreign tax credit — so once home-country tax is added in, a foreign winner very often ends up keeping less than a US resident would.

Why do China and India residents keep more or less than Korea residents?

China taxes lottery winnings as incidental income at a flat 20%, and that's already lower than the 30% the US withholds up front — so the foreign tax credit wipes out any additional China tax owed, and a China resident keeps the most of the four. India applies a flat 30% rate plus a surcharge and cess that push the effective rate to about 39%, slightly above the US withholding, so an India resident owes a bit more on top and keeps less than a China resident but more than a Korea resident (who faces Korea's progressive income tax up to 45%).
